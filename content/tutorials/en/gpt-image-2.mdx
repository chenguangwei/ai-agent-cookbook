## 4. Korean Girl Group 3x3 Photobook (Consistency)

This category spread fastest in the X idol/photography circles. A 3x3 grid of nine poses of the same idol, with consistent facial features, hairstyle, and outfit, soft lighting + film grain, like a real photobook.

Complete prompt (copy directly):

```text
9:16 vertical — a 3x3 grid collage (nine images) forming a Korean idol portrait photoshoot series. Each frame features the same young Korean female idol, maintaining 100% consistency in facial features, proportions, hairstyle, and identity across all nine shots. Natural, ultra-realistic skin texture, no retouching, no smoothing. Clean idol-style minimal makeup, soft glow, subtle imperfections. Hair: long, voluminous dark hair, slightly tousled, consistent across all frames. Outfit: cohesive Korean idol photoshoot styling — white shirt + short bottoms, same outfit across all frames. Setting: minimal studio or simple indoor environment, plain wall, soft window light, clean background. Lighting: soft diffused natural light, gentle highlights, low contrast, subtle film-like softness. Frame breakdown (3x3 grid): Top row — standing naturally looking slightly away / facing camera casual mid-motion / slight side angle soft gaze. Middle row — looking slightly upward / close-up direct eye contact gentle smile / turning body slightly mid-motion. Bottom row — seated or leaning casually / back partially turned looking over shoulder / standing close to frame soft expression. Mood: Korean idol photobook aesthetic, intimate, soft, natural. Quality: ultra-realistic, 8K detail, subtle analog film grain, natural imperfections, soft dreamy tone.
```

The technical key here is the phrase "100% consistency"—it tells the model that all nine images are the same person. Removing it will result in nine different faces.

## VI. Short Drama Storyboard (Same Character × 12 Panels, Short Drama/Novel Illustration Fans Are Already Copying Like Crazy)

The fourth category demonstrated "the same person in a 9-grid layout"; this category is: placing the same protagonist in 12 different scenes as a storyboard. In the past, hiring a storyboard artist to draw 12 panels of a consistent character cost thousands per job; now, one prompt generates 12 panels.

This category is already spreading rapidly among short drama/AI film/novel illustration circles on X. The version below is a 12-panel storyboard example for a female protagonist in a post-apocalyptic survival theme (divided into 4 groups of 3 panels each). You can completely replace the protagonist's setting and storyline while reusing the structure.

Full prompt (copy directly):

```text
Generate a set of 12-panel short drama storyboard illustrations (4 rows × 3 columns, each panel numbered 1-12), cinematic realistic illustration style, film grain, cold-toned rainy night, post-apocalyptic wasteland atmosphere. All 12 panels must maintain 100% consistency in the female protagonist's face, hairstyle, body type, and clothing (ultra-consistent character identity across all 12 frames). Protagonist setting: Asian female, in her 20s, long black hair half-tied back, dirty and worn dark gray work jacket + tactical pants + combat boots, face with traces of rain and dust, eyes tired but determined, left arm bandaged.

Storyboard 1-3: Searching for Hope
1. She stands outside an abandoned subway station, looking down at an old-fashioned phone trying to find an emergency broadcast signal, the phone's faint light illuminating her face.
2. She enters a dilapidated convenience store with collapsed shelves and shattered glass, searching for unexpired bottled water and supplies.
3. Her reflection is visible in the convenience store's glass door, her tired expression clear, a neon sign flickering faintly in the rain outside.

Storyboard 4-6: Difficult Return and Supply Inventory
4. She stops at a rain-soaked crosswalk, the red light on, the street empty, streetlights reflecting in the puddles.
5. Back at the shelter, she leans her wet umbrella against the door, shoulders drooping, showing the fatigue of just entering.
6. Under the dim candlelight, she sits cross-legged on the floor, carefully counting the few cans of food and one bottle of water she brought back, a rough cloth spread on the table.

Storyboard 7-9: Information, Injury, and Planning
7. She tends to an old-fashioned radio, leaning her ear close to the speaker, searching for external signals amidst the static.
8. She bows her head to carefully treat the wound on her left arm, bandages and disinfectant placed nearby, her expression stoic.
9. Under the faint light of a flashlight, she leans over to study a spread-out city map, her finger tracing an evacuation route.

Storyboard 10-12: Alertness and Setting Off Again
10. Late at night, a sound outside the window makes her instantly alert; she grabs a weapon (pistol or short baton), her eyes cold and wary.
11. She stands by the window, quietly gazing at the desolate, wet wasteland outside, the silhouettes of collapsed buildings in the distance.
12. In the faint morning light, she puts on a heavy backpack and a gas mask, stepping into the drizzle to continue her journey, her back showing determination.

Requirements: Each panel has a full composition, cinematic lens language (alternating close-up/mid-shot/long shot), cool color palette (dark green, gray-blue, rust orange accents), strong contrast, clear depiction of rain and wet textures. The protagonist's clothing, hairstyle, wound location, and facial features must be consistent throughout. Uniform layout of 4 rows × 3 columns, separated by thin white lines between panels, with storyboard numbers 01-12 in the top left corner, no extra text, no watermark. Image ratio 3:4, overall horizontal layout.
```

The key to this structure lies in three things:

1. The opening line "ultra-consistent character identity across all 12 frames" — this is the core for keeping the face, hairstyle, and clothing consistent across all 12 panels.
2. The protagonist setting is a separate paragraph — describing "what they look like, what they wear, what injuries they have" all at once, so there's no need to repeat it in each panel description.
3. The storyboard is written in 4 groups × 3 panels — each group has a small narrative unit (Searching / Return / Planning / Setting Off), making the 12 panels feel like a real short drama title sequence, not a random collage.

How to adapt it for your own short drama: Replace the "post-apocalyptic wasteland + female survival" shell (e.g., campus romance / suspense crime / ancient martial arts / sci-fi space), change the protagonist setting paragraph, rewrite the 12 panel scenes, and keep all other sentence structures. One template can generate ten title sequences for a short drama series.

### 8.1 Public Building A1 Presentation Board (Qingdao Seaside Art Museum Example)

A university architecture A1 presentation board typically takes 2-3 days to make: renderings + site plan + plans/elevations/sections + diagrams + exploded axonometric + design description, requiring six to eight types of graphics on one sheet. Now, one prompt generates it directly.

Full prompt (copy directly):

```text
1. Generate an image of a public building rendering, a large art museum, "High-tech" + "Modernist", the building is located by the sea in Qingdao city, high-angle bird's-eye view, lighting effect at 3 PM in the afternoon.

2. Generate an A1 presentation board for this building, including all the necessary analysis diagram types and analysis modules that should be on a presentation board.
```

> How to adapt: Simply replace the city, site, building type (museum / library / train station / community center / visitor center), and design language (Brutalism / New Chinese Style / Parametric / Deconstructivism); the four-grid analysis modules (Location / Circulation / Massing / Function) are standard architectural curriculum conventions, keep them unchanged. This template is enough for an architecture student to use for a whole semester.

## ! A Reminder:

It's not surprising that a model can generate impressive images, but it's a new problem when it makes people doubt the "ordinary pictures" they see every day.

In the past, creating fake documents required PS skills, font libraries, and paper scanning; making fake live-stream screenshots required cropping and compositing. Now, a few prompts are enough.

AI is already incredibly powerful and accessible. There's no way to stop anyone from using it, but as someone who writes content, I also want to give a warning: seeing is not necessarily believing.
